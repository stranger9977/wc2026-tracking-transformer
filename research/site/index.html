What is chemistry, and can we see it?

Chemistry is the unspoken understanding between teammates — the run a passer trusts without looking, the cover a defender starts before his partner steps. Geir Jordet's scanning research shows it isn't telepathy; it's faster prediction. The 2022 FIFA Men's World Cup gives us 64 matches across 32 squads to ask: can we measure that prediction, see it happen on the pitch, and figure out how it develops?

The definitional tour — 12 named mechanisms (Third-Man Triangle, Decoy Run, the Meat Wall) — lives on What is Chemistry?. Below: how we measure it, show it, and where the data says it comes from.

Strength: catches off-ball runs that move the probability before any event fires. Powers Transformer Chemistry (per-team attention networks) and Interactive Plays (clip-by-clip scrubber).

Event-level for the season-long leaderboard. Frame-level to see a chemistry edge happen. Both share one definition: the part of expected goal difference that depends on which teammates are configured together at this moment. The formal math is on Methodology.

The two metrics, in plain English

Before the formulas: every on-ball action — a pass, a dribble, a tackle, a shot — gets a VAEP score from a model that asks "by how much did this action change my team's expected goal difference over the next 10 actions?" A clever through-ball that sets up a chance gets a big positive number; a sloppy giveaway in midfield gets a small negative one. VAEP turns the whole match into a stream of action-values. JOI and JDI are just two ways of pooling those action-values by pair instead of by player.

JOI Joint Offensive Impact

Plain English. JOI sums VAEP across pairs of teammates whose actions touch each other — P passes, Q receives. It asks: "when these two play together, does the ball flow through them productively?" A high JOI pair lights up every time their consecutive on-ball touches build something.

Normalized per 90 minutes shared on the pitch (JOI90) so pairs with different playing time are comparable.

The math
\( \mathrm{JOI}(p, q) = \sum_{(a_i, a_{i+1}) \in S_{p,q}} V(a_i) + V(a_{i+1}) \)

\( S_{p,q} \) is the set of consecutive same-team action pairs where one belongs to \( p \) and the next to \( q \) (or vice versa). \( V \) is the VAEP value of that action.

JDI Joint Defensive Impact

Plain English. JDI credits a pair when the opponents they share defensive responsibility for under-perform their expected VAEP. It asks: "when these two are on the pitch together, do the attackers they're tasked with stopping get fewer dangerous touches than usual?" The credit for an opponent's missing output is split across the pairs nearest him on the pitch.

Same per-90 normalization as JOI (JDI90).

The math
\( \mathrm{JDI}_m(p, q) = \sum_o \big( \mathbb{E}[\mathrm{OI}_o] - \mathrm{OI}_o \big) \cdot R_m(p, q, o) \cdot \tfrac{\mathrm{mins}_m(p,q,o)}{90} \)

\( R_m(p,q,o) = \tfrac{1}{2}\big(r_p(o) + r_q(o)\big) \) and \( r_p(o) \propto 1/d(p,o) \) in the (mirrored) 5×5 grid. \( \mathbb{E}[\mathrm{OI}_o] \) is the opponent's expected offensive impact per minute, modeled across the corpus.

The 5×5 responsibility grid

Defensive responsibility is split into 25 zones (5 horizontal × 5 vertical bands of the pitch). For each zone, we compute each player's share of defensive actions while they were on the pitch. JDI rewards pairs that jointly suppress more opponent threat than expected in zones where both players are responsible.
