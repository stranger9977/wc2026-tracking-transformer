Chemistry leaderboards

Six views of chemistry, in one place. Event-based JOI/JDI ranks pairs by joint on-ball impact (VAEP across consecutive on-ball actions). Attention-based and When scoring vs neutral rank pairs by the tracking transformer's off-ball attention. AW-JOI is the continuous, value-weighted version of attention chemistry — for every frame, attention coupling × ΔP(score). The Nucleus networks view re-anchors all this around individual players. Where they disagree surfaces pairs the lenses argue about.

JOI vs AW-JOI in one line. JOI fires only when both teammates touch the ball (passing / dribble chemistry). AW-JOI fires every frame the model watches both together (positional / off-ball chemistry). Same family, different aperture.

How to read this

For every pair present in both datasets, we compute its percentile rank on JOI90 (event-based) and on attention lift (transformer-based), then score the gap as |pct(JOI) − pct(attention)|. The top rows are pairs the model watches together but rarely connect on the ball, or vice versa — the off-ball vs on-ball gap. GK pairs are excluded so the ranking isn't dominated by the goalkeeper's universal touch.

The headline: Wales' Ethan Ampadu + Joe Allen (both CM) rank p100 on attention lift but only p18 on JOI — the transformer watches them constantly but they barely combine on the ball. That's the cleanest off-ball-only chemistry signal in the dataset. The Welsh double-pivot shaped the build-up without touching it.

Percentiles are computed inside the dataset that produced each metric, then joined by team + unordered player-pair. A pair only appears here if it has a non-zero attention record and survives the event-based minimum minutes filter on this page.

These are the pairs the model watches above its own baseline specifically when scoring is imminent. The off-off category isolates offensive chemistry from the GK / defender attention bias that dominates the raw leaderboard.

Powered by the score-specialist transformer — a single-head model trained only on P(score). Replaces the shared two-head model deployed previously after an A/B showed 85% top-20 turnover in the off-off pairs (the specialist surfaces named attacking partnerships across Japan, Portugal, Argentina, Germany and Cameroon that the shared model didn't). See Methodology §2.3.1.

For every pair we split tracking frames into three buckets — score (team scores within 10 s), concede (concedes within 10 s), and neutral (everything else) — then compute mean attention per frame in each bucket. The lift column is score-frame attn / neutral-frame attn: pairs the transformer attends to relatively more when goals are coming.

Frame buckets come from the frame-VAEP label scheme (10 s look-ahead). Pair requires ≥ 60 min together and ≥ 50 frames in both score and neutral buckets to qualify.

The continuous, value-weighted version of attention chemistry. For every frame, we credit a pair by the product of the two players' ball→player attentions times the change in P(score) − P(concede) between consecutive frames. Sum across the whole tournament, normalize per 90 minutes shared.

AW-JOI credits only positive-value frames (build-up + scoring chances). AW-JDI credits negative-value frames (collapses + chances against). Net = AW-JOI − AW-JDI is the headline chemistry value. Attention from the score-specialist for AW-JOI; concede-specialist for AW-JDI. See Methodology §2.3.1.

This metric is the frame-level analogue of event-based JOI/JDI — but with attention as the pair coupling instead of "consecutive on-ball actions." It fires every frame (continuous), it weights by the model's "who matters here" judgment, and it's sign-correct. The conditional "When scoring vs neutral" view uses a binary score/neutral bucket cutoff; AW-JOI replaces that with a continuous integral. Two views of the same underlying signal — bucketed and continuous.

Pair requires ≥ 60 minutes together. AW-JOI90 + AW-JDI90 are normalized per 90 minutes shared on the pitch.

Each player's chemistry network, anchored at them. For every WC22 player we aggregate their AW-JOI across every teammate they shared ≥ 120 minutes with — their network. Five different ways to slice that network are in the sort dropdown; each tells a different story. Click a player row to see the star graph — nucleus in the centre, top-8 spokes radiating out, edge thickness ∝ AW-JOI90.

What each sort surfaces
  • Network AW-JOI90 — per-90 average density. Surfaces star wingers + connective fullbacks (Vinícius, Raphinha, Mbappé, Acuña). Penalizes long-minute players.
  • # strong partners — count of teammates with AW-JOI90 ≥ 0.4. The breadth view. Messi ranks #3 here (14 strong partners), alongside Tchouaméni, Perišić, Varane, Gvardiol, Ziyech, Hakimi, Modrić — players who connected with their entire squad.
  • Top-5 spokes sum — best 5 connections only. Caps long tails. Surfaces players with concentrated chemistry trios.
  • Total AW-JOI sum — raw accumulation, not per-90. Rewards long-minute hub players: Mbappé, Theo Hernandez, Perišić, Messi (#4).
  • Team-relative lift — player's AW-JOI90 / team mean. Surfaces players who stand out within their squad. Dalot (POR), Foden (ENG), Walker (ENG), Attiat-Allah (MAR) lead.

Goalkeepers excluded from the nucleus list (they trivially connect to everyone). Min 200 min played to qualify. Spokes use ≥ 120 min pairs only. Spokes CAN include GKs — Messi's top partner is Emiliano Martínez (1.80 AW-JOI90), Argentina's build-from-back signal.

How healthy is each team's chemistry network as a whole? For every team we count how many of their same-team pairs cross the strong-chemistry bar (AW-JOI90 ≥ 0.4) — split into offensive, defensive, and cross-line pairs — plus the mean and the Gini coefficient of the AW-JOI90 distribution (lower = more evenly distributed across pairs).

The headline: the top 4 teams by total strong pairs are exactly the four semifinalists, in finish order — France (86), Croatia (84), Argentina (82), Morocco (76). And all four have Gini 0.28–0.30 — very even distribution. Concentrated chemistry (high Gini, fewer pairs) loses to distributed chemistry (low Gini, more pairs). Brazil illustrates the trade-off: highest mean pair strength (1.00) but only 69 strong pairs total — exited QF.
