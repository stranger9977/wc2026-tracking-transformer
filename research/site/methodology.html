Methodology

Full math, modelling choices, and limitations. This recreates Bransen & Van Haaren (MIT Sloan, 2020) using PFF event data from the 2022 FIFA World Cup, then extends it with a tracking-data transformer that sees all 22 players at every frame. References to "the paper" point to Bransen & Van Haaren.

What we measure, in plain English

The math below is real, but you don't need it to follow the site. Here's the whole pipeline in four short paragraphs.

VAEP — how much each on-ball action was worth

Every pass, dribble, cross, take-on, and shot in the World Cup is fed into a model that asks two questions: "after this action, how likely is the team in possession to score in the next few seconds?" and "how likely are they to concede?" The action's VAEP value is the change in expected goal difference it produced — positive if it improved the team's situation, negative if it made things worse. Goals get big positive numbers; backpasses under pressure get small negative ones. It's the unit of currency for everything that follows.

JOI / JDI — credit-sharing between teammates

Joint Offensive Impact (JOI) credits a same-team pair whenever they touch the ball in back-to-back actions: both VAEP values flow into the pair's JOI total. Joint Defensive Impact (JDI) is the harder half — event data can't see who marked whom, so we instead reward pairs whose opponents under-performed their expected offensive impact, weighted by how close each defender stood to each opponent (a 5×5 grid heuristic). Both totals are normalized by minutes the pair shared on the pitch — JOI90 and JDI90 are the units you see on every leaderboard.

Attention chemistry — what the model "watches" off the ball

Event data only sees the player who touched the ball. To get at the off-ball half of chemistry — the runs that pull defenders, the teammate-aware positioning — we trained a transformer on tracking data (all 22 players, 5 frames per second). The model learns to predict the team in possession's frame-VAEP score and concede probabilities. As a free byproduct it produces an attention map that says, for every frame, which players the model needed to look at to make its prediction. We sum per-pair attention across the match, subtract a ball-distance baseline (the average attention a random pair gets at the same mean-distance-to-ball, so that defenders near the ball during set pieces aren't double-counted as "chemistry"), then normalize by each team's 75th-percentile pair to get the attention-chemistry lift. A pair with a lift of 2.0 is twice as "co-watched" by the model as a typical above-mean pair on its own team.

The Digital Whiteboard — counterfactual swaps

The leaderboards tell you who clicked. The Whiteboard asks "what if?" — swap one player for another on the same team, run the transformer again, and see whether the predicted scoring probability over a real possession sequence goes up or down. It's a tactical sandbox built on the same frame-VAEP heads described below.

1. Event-based pipeline (VAEP / JOI / JDI)

Every on-ball event in PFF is converted to SPADL (Soccer Player Action Description Language) form. We then train two gradient-boosted classifiers over a 3-action context window:

  • P-score: probability the possessing team scores within the next 10 actions.
  • P-concede: probability the possessing team concedes within the next 10 actions.

VAEP action value for action \(a\) is the change in expected goal difference it produces:

\[ V(a) = \big( P_{\mathrm{score}}(s_a) - P_{\mathrm{score}}(s_{a-1}) \big) - \big( P_{\mathrm{concede}}(s_a) - P_{\mathrm{concede}}(s_{a-1}) \big) \]

# swap from the current team's perspective).
same = (teams == prev_team)
prev_value_self_persp = np.where(same, prev_pS - prev_pC, prev_pC - prev_pS)
df["vaep_value"] = (pS - pC) - prev_value_self_persp

Joint Offensive Impact (JOI)

For an ordered pair of consecutive actions \((a_i, a_{i+1})\) by teammates \(p\) and \(q\), both VAEP values contribute to JOI(p, q). We sum across all such interactions in the corpus.

\[ \mathrm{JOI}(p, q) = \sum_{(a_i, a_{i+1}) \in S_{p, q}} V(a_i) + V(a_{i+1}) \]
Python implementation (excerpt) 
# research/src/chemistry/joint/joi.py
INTERACTION_TYPES = {"pass", "cross", "dribble", "take_on", "shot"}
for i in range(n - 1):
    if types[i] not in INTERACTION_TYPES or types[i+1] not in INTERACTION_TYPES:
        continue
    if teams[i] != teams[i+1] or players[i] == players[i+1]:
        continue
    lo, hi = sorted((players[i], players[i+1]))
    pair_sum[(lo, hi)] += vaeps[i] + vaeps[i+1]

# Per-90 normalization
joi90 = joi * 90.0 / minutes_together

JOI is normalized by minutes the pair shared on the pitch: \( \mathrm{JOI90}(p,q) = 90 \cdot \mathrm{JOI}(p,q) / m_{p,q} \).

Joint Defensive Impact (JDI)

Match event data describes the actions that happened, not the actions a defender prevented. JDI sidesteps this by giving credit to a same-team pair when their opponents underperform their expected offensive impact. For every opposing player \(o\) the pair faced, we compute the gap between \(o\)'s expected and actual OI and divide it across same-team pairs by a responsibility share derived from the 5×5 position grid.

Per-match offensive impact of player \(o\):

\[ \mathrm{OI}_m(o) \;=\; \sum_{a \in \{\text{pass, cross, dribble, take-on, shot}\}} V(a) \]

Expected OI uses player \(o\)'s per-90 mean across prior matches in this dataset, Bayesian-shrunk toward a positional prior (GK / DEF / MID / FWD) when fewer than \(M_0 = 700\) minutes have been played.

Responsibility share. Each on-pitch teammate has individual share \( r_p(o) = (1/d(p, o)) \,/\, \sum_q (1/d(q, o)) \), where \(d\) is Euclidean distance in the 5×5 grid after mirroring the opponent's cell into our coordinate frame (so our defenders land near their forwards). The pair share is the simple mean \( R_m(p, q, o) = \tfrac{1}{2}\big(r_p(o) + r_q(o)\big) \).

Per-match JDI:

\[ \mathrm{JDI}_m(p, q) \;=\; \sum_o \big( \mathbb{E}[\mathrm{OI}_m(o)] - \mathrm{OI}_m(o) \big) \cdot R_m(p, q, o) \cdot \frac{\mathrm{mins}_m(p, q, o)}{90} \]
Python implementation (excerpt) 
# research/src/chemistry/joint/jdi.py
def _mirror_position(pos):
    r, c = grid_cell(pos)               # 0..5 rows, 0..4 cols
    return ((4 - r) if r <= 4 else 5, 4 - c)

def _responsibility_per_opponent(team_players, opponent_pos):
    distances = {p.id: 1.0 / (grid_distance_vs_opponent(p.pos, opponent_pos) + 0.5)
                 for p in team_players}
    total = sum(distances.values())
    indiv = {pid: w / total for pid, w in distances.items()}
    # pair share = (s_p + s_q) / 2
    return {(p, q): (indiv[p] + indiv[q]) / 2 for p, q in pairs(team_players)}

def jdi_m(pair_p, pair_q, opponents):
    return sum(
        (E_OI[o] - OI[o]) * RESP[(pair_p, pair_q, o)] * mins_m[(pair_p, pair_q, o)] / 90
        for o in opponents
    )

Aggregated across matches and normalized per 90 shared minutes gives JDI90.

2. Tracking transformer (frame-VAEP heads)

Event data is blind to the 21 players not currently touching the ball. To recover the off-ball half of chemistry we trained a transformer on PFF tracking data, downsampled to 5 Hz, that takes a single frame as input and predicts a frame-level analogue of the VAEP score / concede probabilities. The architecture is adapted from SumerSports's SportsTrackingTransformer (NFL), with three changes: soccer geometry, two BCE heads instead of an NFL-specific target, and an attention-extraction path that's a first-class output, not a debug hook.

2.1 Input representation

Every frame is a 23-token sequence: 22 players + 1 ball. Each token is a 7-dimensional feature vector:

  • x_norm, y_norm — pitch position, centered and scaled to \([-1, 1]\)
  • vx, vy — velocity from finite differences, clamped at ±25 m/s
  • is_attacking_side — 1 if the token belongs to the team in possession
  • is_goalkeeper — 1 if GK (identified via the extreme-x heuristic)
  • has_possession — 1 if this token is currently the ball carrier (or is the ball)

Player ordering within the block carries no meaning; the transformer is permutation-equivariant over tokens. Velocity uses stride-aware \(\Delta t\) so sources at different native frame rates (Metrica 25 Hz, SkillCorner 10 Hz, PFF 30 Hz) all land on a common 5 Hz grid.

2.2 Architecture

A thin stack of standard transformer blocks:

\[ \text{Input}\ (B, 23, 7) \;\to\; \mathrm{BatchNorm_{features}} \;\to\; \mathrm{Linear}\ (7 \to d) \;\to\; \big[\mathrm{TransformerEncoderLayer}(d, h)\big]^{L} \;\to\; \mathrm{Heads} \]

Encoder layers use pre-norm ordering and GELU activations. Two parallel sigmoid heads sit on the pooled output:

  • \(P_{\mathrm{score}}^{\mathrm{frame}}\): probability the team currently in possession scores within the next 10 seconds.
  • \(P_{\mathrm{concede}}^{\mathrm{frame}}\): probability the team currently in possession concedes within the next 10 seconds.

The frame-VAEP value is the same difference as the event-data version, applied frame-by-frame: \( V^{\mathrm{frame}}(t) = P_{\mathrm{score}}^{\mathrm{frame}}(t) - P_{\mathrm{concede}}^{\mathrm{frame}}(t) \).

2.3 Training

44 PFF World Cup '22 matches split 36 train / 8 val. Class-weighted BCE on both heads (positives are rare — < 4% of frames precede a goal within 10 s). Best-epoch validation metrics:

  • \(P_{\mathrm{score}}^{\mathrm{frame}}\) val AUC ≈ 0.80, Brier ≈ 0.187
  • \(P_{\mathrm{concede}}^{\mathrm{frame}}\) val AUC ≈ 0.79, Brier ≈ 0.069

The site's attention chemistry uses the frame-VAEP checkpoint described above — the model predicts \(P_{\mathrm{score}}\) / \(P_{\mathrm{concede}}\) directly, not an xT proxy. The checkpoint is downloadable from the Downloads page.

2.3.1 Two-specialist experiment

The shared two-head backbone attends disproportionately to GKs and defenders because P(concede) carries tighter spatial signal than P(score) and dominates the gradient. To test whether a dedicated offensive representation surfaces cleaner chemistry, we trained two single-head specialists — score-only and concede-only — on the same backbone (2 layers × 4 heads × 64 dim), same 36/8 split, same class weighting, 6 epochs each.

  • Score specialist val AUC: 0.816 (shared model: 0.801, +0.015).
  • Concede specialist val AUC: 0.799 (shared model: 0.792, +0.007).

Both specialists modestly beat the shared model on their respective head. We then re-extracted per-pair attention chemistry from each and compared with the shared-model pipeline. Findings:

  • The score specialist's top-10 off-off pairs overlap the shared model's by 7/10. New entries surfaced include Dembélé + Mbappé at #1 (was #7 on shared) and Vinícius + Raphinha (Brazil's Real Madrid duo, not in the shared top-10 at all).
  • The conditional view (frames that lead to a goal in the next 10 s) already does most of the interpretive work. The shared-model conditional leaderboard and the specialist conditional leaderboard surface largely the same pairs — Morocco's Ziyech / Ounahi / En-Nesyri family at the top either way.
  • The PSG triangle (Argentina's Messi + Di María + Paredes) had JOI90 = 0.33 for Messi–Di María, by far the highest pair in the squad. The score specialist's tracking attention shows the triangle at ratio 0.92× the team baseline — below average. The "rehearsal carries over" pattern is event-bound (JOI), not visible in off-ball attention even after specialising on the offensive head.
  • Germany Bayern is the cleanest reversal: it was the worst cluster on JOI (Δ JOI90 = −0.101) but the only nation with a significant positive shared-history signal on the score-specialist's attention (Δ attention = +471, Mann-Whitney p < 0.001). See Club v National for the full multi-act write-up.

Specialist checkpoints (transformer_score_only.ckpt, transformer_concede_only.ckpt) are downloadable from the Downloads page. Per-pair attention parquets ship under research/data/ with _score_specialist / _concede_specialist suffixes.

2.4 Attention extraction

The transformer exposes an encode_with_attention path that returns the pooled encoding alongside every layer's per-head attention tensor:

\[ \mathrm{Attn} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times L \times H \times T \times T}, \quad T = 23 \]

For chemistry we collapse layers and heads by sum, then take the row corresponding to the ball token — i.e. for every frame, "how much of the model's prediction depended on each of the 22 players?" That row, summed over a possession sequence, is the raw ingredient for the pair-chemistry score.

Python implementation (excerpt) 
# src/wc2026_tracking_transformer/model/transformer.py
def encode_with_attention(self, x):
    # x: (B, 23, 7)
    h = self.feature_norm(x)
    h = self.embed(h)                          # (B, 23, d)
    attns = []
    for layer in self.encoder_layers:
        h, attn = layer(h, need_weights=True)  # attn: (B, H, T, T)
        attns.append(attn)
    attn_stack = torch.stack(attns, dim=1)     # (B, L, H, T, T)
    return h, attn_stack

3. Attention chemistry aggregation

Given the per-frame ball-token attention vector, we want a single "co-watched-ness" score per ordered pair \((p, q)\) on the same team. The recipe:

3.1 Sum ball-token attention by pair

Let \(\alpha_t(i)\) be the ball-token's attention weight to player token \(i\) at frame \(t\), summed across layers and heads. For every frame in which both \(p\) and \(q\) are on the pitch on the team in possession, we add the product of their attention to the pair:

\[ A(p, q) \;=\; \sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}_{p,q}} \alpha_t(p) \cdot \alpha_t(q) \]

The product (not sum) is what makes this a chemistry signal: a pair only scores high when the model is looking at both of them in the same instant.

3.2 Ball-distance baseline subtraction

Raw \(A(p, q)\) is dominated by pairs that happen to sit close to the ball a lot — goalkeepers during defensive sequences, centre-backs in build-up. To isolate the chemistry signal from the geography signal, we condition on the pair's mean Euclidean distance to the ball. Let \(d_t(p, q) = \tfrac{1}{2}(\|x_t(p) - x_t(b)\| + \|x_t(q) - x_t(b)\|)\), bin into ten 5-meter buckets (last bucket open at 45m+), and let \(\hat{\mu}_b\) be the average pair-attention across the entire corpus inside bin \(b\):

\[ \hat{\mu}_b \;=\; \frac{\sum_{(t, p, q)\ :\ d_t(p,q) \in b} \alpha_t(p, q)} {\#\{(t, p, q) : d_t(p, q) \in b\}} \]

The pair's expected attention is the dot-product of its per-bin frame counts with the corpus baseline, and the baseline-corrected pair-attention is the difference between observed and expected:

\[ \tilde{A}(p, q) \;=\; A(p, q) \;-\; \sum_{b} n_b(p, q) \cdot \hat{\mu}_b \]

\(\tilde{A}(p, q) > 0\) means the model attended to this pair more than a random pair would at the same ball-distance profile; \(\tilde{A}(p, q) < 0\) means less. Every downstream aggregate — per-90, lift, group score — uses \(\tilde{A}\) instead of \(A\), which is what removes the GK-and-build-up dominance from the leaderboards.

Concretely: ten distance bins span 0–45m in 5m steps (the tenth bin is open at 45m+). For each accepted frame we compute every pair's mean Euclidean distance to the ball and add its attention into the matching bin; the per-bin corpus baseline is the unweighted mean of every (pair, frame) contribution that landed in that bin across all 44 PFF matches. A pair's expected attention is its per-bin frame counts dotted with this corpus-wide baseline vector, and the published number is the raw sum minus that expected value. The per-bin baseline rises monotonically with distance (0.063 in bin 0, 0.164 in bin 9), so deep CBs and GKs — who tend to live far from the ball — give back proportionally more.

3.3 Per-90 normalization

Pairs with more shared minutes accumulate more attention by default, so we normalize identically to JOI / JDI:

\[ A_{90}(p, q) \;=\; 90 \cdot \frac{\tilde{A}(p, q)}{m_{p, q}} \]

3.4 Team-baseline lift (PMI-style)

Different teams play different volumes of possession, so raw \(A_{90}\) isn't comparable across squads. We compute a lift against each team's 75th-percentile pair — analogous to pointwise mutual information against the team's "above-mean background distribution" of attention. (The 75th percentile rather than the median because \(\tilde{A}_{90}\) is now centered near zero by construction — a divide-by-median would explode for teams whose median pair sits near baseline.)

\[ \mathrm{lift}(p, q) \;=\; \frac{\tilde{A}_{90}(p, q)}{Q_{0.75,\ (p', q') \in \mathrm{team}}\ \tilde{A}_{90}(p', q')} \]

A lift of 1.0 is a typical above-mean pair on its team; 2.0 is twice that; high values are the kind of automatism that shows up in highlight reels.

Python implementation (excerpt) 
# research/scripts/build_attention_chemistry.py
def pair_attention(attn_stack, frames_meta):
    # attn_stack: (T_frames, L, H, 23, 23)
    # Sum layers + heads, take ball-token row.
    a = attn_stack.sum(dim=(1, 2))[:, 0, 1:]    # (T_frames, 22)
    out = defaultdict(float)
    for t, row in enumerate(a):
        team = frames_meta[t]["team_in_possession"]
        for i, j in pairs_on_pitch(t, team):
            out[(i, j)] += float(row[i] * row[j])
    return out

def lift(per90, team_to_pairs):
    out = {}
    for team, pair_list in team_to_pairs.items():
        med = np.median([per90[p] for p in pair_list]) or 1e-9
        for p in pair_list:
            out[p] = per90[p] / med
    return out

3.5 Triples and quads

The same machinery extends to groups of three or four — the leaderboard's "groups" tab shows top-attention triangles and box-of-four sub-formations. For a group \(G\) the score is the average pairwise \(A_{90}\) inside it:

\[ A_{90}(G) \;=\; \frac{1}{\binom{|G|}{2}} \sum_{\{p, q\} \subset G} A_{90}(p, q) \]

and lift is normalized by the team's median pair as above. See chemistry leaderboards for the live tables.

4. Team builder (mixed-integer program)

Given pair-chemistry scores for every two-player combination in a squad, Bransen's optimal-XI selection is a 0/1 MIP:

\[ \max_{x \in \{0,1\}^{|P|}} \sum_{p \lt q} \mathrm{Chem}(p, q) \cdot x_p x_q \] \[ \text{s.t.} \quad \sum_p x_p = 11, \quad \sum_{p \in \mathrm{GK}} x_p = 1, \quad \sum_{p \in \mathrm{DEF}} x_p = n_{\mathrm{DEF}}, \ldots \]

The product \(x_p x_q\) is linearized via a standard pair-indicator auxiliary variable \(y_{pq} \in [0,1]\) with constraints \(y_{pq} \le x_p,\ y_{pq} \le x_q,\ y_{pq} \ge x_p + x_q - 1\).

Python implementation (excerpt) 
# research/src/chemistry/teambuilder/builder.py
# Formation-enumeration variant of Bransen's MIP.
FORMATIONS = {
    "4-3-3":   {"GK": 1, "DEF": 4, "MID": 3, "FWD": 3},
    "4-4-2":   {"GK": 1, "DEF": 4, "MID": 4, "FWD": 2},
    "3-5-2":   {"GK": 1, "DEF": 3, "MID": 5, "FWD": 2},
    "3-4-3":   {"GK": 1, "DEF": 3, "MID": 4, "FWD": 3},
    "4-2-3-1": {"GK": 1, "DEF": 4, "MID": 5, "FWD": 1},
}

# Pair value combines offense + defense by user-chosen alpha
pair_value[(p, q)] = alpha * joi90[(p, q)] + (1 - alpha) * jdi90[(p, q)]

best = None
for fname, slots in FORMATIONS.items():
    for gk in combinations(by_role["GK"], slots["GK"]):
      for d in combinations(by_role["DEF"], slots["DEF"]):
        for m in combinations(by_role["MID"], slots["MID"]):
          for f in combinations(by_role["FWD"], slots["FWD"]):
              team = list(gk) + list(d) + list(m) + list(f)
              score = sum(pair_value.get((min(a,b), max(a,b)), 0.0)
                          for a, b in combinations(team, 2))
              if best is None or score > best["score"]:
                  best = {"formation": fname, "score": score, "players": team}

5. Limitations & provider calibration

  • Tournament-only baseline. All "expected" values — the Bayesian-shrunk E[OI] used inside JDI, and the per-player Δ OI/90 shown on the Overview — are computed from this 64-match corpus alone. Most players have 2–4 matches here, so the per-player prior collapses to a positional average. That makes our "form" delta a tournament-relative gauge, not a club-vs-national comparison. The Club vs National tab closes part of that loop using StatsBomb open data.
  • Provider calibration. WC22 chemistry numbers come from our PFF event + tracking pipeline; the cross-context data on Club vs National is StatsBomb event data passed through the same VAEP model. Action-coverage and tagging conventions differ slightly between providers, so treat the delta as directional, not absolute. The PFF WC22 vs StatsBomb WC22 row pair is the calibration check — if those agree for a player, we trust the other rows too.
  • Small sample. 64 matches across 32 teams means many pairs share well under 90 minutes. We apply a minimum-minutes filter on the Top Pairs tables to mitigate, but JDI90 in particular is noisy at low sample. Attention chemistry uses the same minimum-minutes gating.
  • Defensive responsibility is a heuristic. Bransen approximates defensive involvement from on-ball recovery / tackle / interception actions in each zone. Off-ball positioning is invisible to event data — the tracking transformer in §2–3 is the structured complement.
  • Attention ≠ causation. A high attention-lift pair means the frame-VAEP model needed to see both players to make its prediction. That's strong evidence of a coordinated tactical pattern, but it isn't a counterfactual claim that swapping one player would change the outcome by a specific number of expected goals. The Whiteboard tool is the place for counterfactuals.
  • VAEP credit-assignment is action-level. A pass that "creates space" but doesn't directly trigger threat is undervalued by VAEP, and JOI/JDI inherit this. Attention chemistry partially escapes this — the model can credit off-ball movement — but the underlying BCE targets are still derived from event outcomes.
  • National-team novelty effect. The very thing we want to study — teams whose players don't play together often — is also the smallest-sample regime. Treat absolute numbers as suggestive, comparisons within a team as more robust.
  • External corroboration of the Morocco finding. One mitigation against the "model is fooling itself" worry is convergence with independent analyses. Benhida et al. (2025, Applied Sciences; in-site summary) run PCA + K-means on FIFA post-match KPIs and identify the same fast-transition attacking axis our score-specialist surfaces in the off-off pair leaderboard (Ziyech / Ounahi / En-Nesyri / Hamdallah / Aboukhlal / Sabiri). Different data, different model class, same conclusion — evidence that the score-frame attention pattern is picking up a real tactical signature rather than a tracking-data artifact.
