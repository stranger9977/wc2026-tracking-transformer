What is chemistry?

Chemistry is what happens between players when the ball isn’t there. It’s the unspoken stuff: a runner who knows the passer’s foot is already cocked, a defender who covers because his partner started to step. Event data sees on-ball moments. Tracking sees everywhere else. This page is what we mean when we say “chemistry.”

Our working definition

Chemistry is the unspoken, repeatable understanding that lets two or more players coordinate spatially without a verbal call — sometimes through rehearsed automatisms, sometimes through emergent peripheral awareness, sometimes through pure pattern-matching from playing together for years.

It’s not telepathy — it’s faster prediction. Geir Jordet’s research at the Norwegian School of Sport Sciences shows Xavi scanned around 0.8 times per second; FIFA World Player winners scan significantly more than peers. The “no-look pass” is mostly an “I-already-looked pass.” What looks like a sixth sense is a receiver who trusts the passer’s last glance registered them, and so makes the run before the ball is hit. Chemistry is the upstream cause of telepathy: a shared, repeatable forecast.

Citations: Sky Sports / Jordet on scanning · Frontiers in Psychology, scanning in EPL footballers.

Does our model see chemistry?

We train a transformer on PFF World Cup 2022 tracking data — all 22 players and the ball, 5 frames per second — to predict max(xT) over the next K seconds. Its attention matrix is (layers, heads, 23, 23): for every pair of tokens, a learned weight at every frame. The question is which of the twelve mechanisms actually show up there.

Strong signal

Pure off-ball mechanics that don’t exist in event data at all — the model is the only thing that could see them.

  • Pinning. The pinned defender and the pinning attacker should attend to each other heavily even when the ball is far away. Pure spatial relationship; tracking-attention territory.
  • Decoy runs. Attention should spike from the would-be receiver to the decoy at the moment the defender takes the bait.
  • Rest-defence anchoring. The holding midfielder should attend to the opposition striker even with the ball in the attacking third.
  • The Meat Wall. On a corner, the screener’s attention should sit on the keeper, not on the ball.

Mixed signal

Event data sees half of these (it sees the pass, not the run). Attention should fill in the missing half.

  • Third-man triangle. Does the on-ball player attend to C before the pass is made? If yes, the model is anticipating; if not, the model is reactive.
  • Overlap / underlap. Easy to find — but the ball sits between the pair, so the ball-token will dominate.
  • Press trap and gegenpressing swarm. Should appear as a cluster of attention onto the opponent ball-carrier from multiple of our players at once.

Weak / contaminated signal

Either the chemistry signal is temporal (lives in several frames we don’t aggregate over) or the ball-token is loud enough to drown it out.

  • Blind pass / scan-and-release. The chemistry is in a 1–2 second pre-scan window. Single-frame attention won’t capture it; we need attention trajectories.
  • Positional rotations. Coordination over 3–5 seconds. Better to look at the change in attention across the rotation window than at any one frame.

The big caveat: ball-token domination

Player tokens mostly attend to the ball — it’s the most informative token in any frame — and player-to-player attention is the residual. Concretely: when Saka is pinning the opponent’s left-back 40 m from the ball while Rice has it in the centre circle, we want the model to attend Saka↔left-back. If it instead just attends Saka↔ball and Rice↔ball, we’ve learned proximity, not chemistry.

Mitigations the analysis uses: compute attention excluding the ball-token, normalise by ball-distance, and compare same-team vs. cross-team pair attention. The Chemistry Leaderboard’s “where they disagree” view is the empirical version of this story — the pairs where attention and JOI/JDI don’t line up are exactly the off-ball chemistry pairs we’re hunting.

The cleanest example in the dataset: Wales’ Ethan Ampadu + Joe Allen rank p100 on attention lift but only p18 on JOI — the transformer watches them constantly but they barely combine on the ball. The Welsh double-pivot shaped the build-up without touching it. That’s exactly the kind of chemistry event data can’t see.

External validation. Benhida et al. (2025, Applied Sciences) reach a converging conclusion about Morocco’s attacking-transition axis using PCA + K-means on FIFA post-match KPIs — independent data, independent method. See the paper summary deck.

How we quantify it

Three measures, three views. They aren’t redundant — they cover three different windows on the same thing.

JOI / JDI Event chemistry

Bransen-style action-level VAEP attributed to consecutive same-team touches. Clean per-pair totals across 64 matches, but only on-ball moments. The full leaderboard lives on Chemistry Leaderboards.

Attention lift Tracking chemistry

For every pair of tokens, the learned attention weight averaged across frames — minus a ball-distance baseline. Catches off-ball relationships the events never see. Per-team maps live on the Chemistry Leaderboard.

Whiteboard Δ Counterfactual chemistry

Drag a player off their actual position by a few metres and re-run the frame-VAEP transformer. The Δ P(score) and Δ P(concede) tell you how load-bearing that one player’s positioning was for the play. Try it on the Digital Whiteboard.
