Chemistry, time, and the WC22 finish order

Football is different. There are no plays — chemistry is being predictable to a teammate and unpredictable to an opponent. We measure that off the ball with frame‑based AW‑JOI (the same metric ranking Messi and Argentina at the top of Team networks), then ask two questions: does chemistry predict tournament finish? and does pre‑tournament shared time predict chemistry?

Headline result. Across 31 WC22 sides, team chemistry density — the count of strong AW‑JOI pairs in each squad — predicts tournament finish at Spearman ρ = +0.76 (p < 0.001). The four densest chemistry networks in the tournament were the four semifinalists, in finish order: France, Croatia, Argentina, Morocco. Brazil (heavy bonds, fewer of them) shows the trade‑off — high per‑pair intensity, narrower distribution, out in the quarters.

Time together matters — somewhat — and chemistry networks that are dense and deep are what help teams win. Coverage‑normalized shared playing history (the share of squad‑pairs who ever played together at club or national level) is a real positive input to chemistry: ρ = +0.37 (p = 0.039) to per‑pair intensity, and ρ = +0.29 (p = 0.11) to overall density. Pairs that have shared a pitch before do build tighter on‑pitch connections in Qatar. But the much bigger effect is structural: squads with denser, deeper chemistry networks — many strong pairs distributed across the lineup — go further, predicting WC22 finish at ρ = +0.76. The four densest networks were the four semifinalists, in finish order.

3. Does prior time together predict chemistry?

Data provenance & caveat. For each WC22 pair we joined PFF's roster history to count overlap_seasons — the number of distinct club or national seasons the two players appeared in the same squad pre‑2022. That estimate is biased by roster‑history coverage — Premier League / Bundesliga / La Liga / Serie A players have near‑complete career trails; players from less‑tracked leagues do not.

To control for this we coverage‑normalize: Ever‑shared % is the share of squad‑pairs where we have club history for both players who ever shared a pitch pre‑WC22. After normalization the team‑level signal: ρ = +0.29 to team chemistry density (strong pairs), p = 0.11; ρ = +0.37 to mean AW‑JOI90, p = 0.039. Real, but suggestive rather than significant. The team chemistry density signal remains the strong one — predicting finish at ρ = +0.76 — and shared rehearsal time is a much weaker input.

Each dot = one squad. X = ever‑shared % (coverage‑normalized). Notice that very high coverage (Spain, England, all 1.00) does not automatically mean high ever‑shared %.

Time matters somewhat. Dense, deep chemistry networks matter a lot.

  • Time has a real positive effect on per‑pair intensity. Pairs that have played together before — at club or national level — build tighter on‑pitch connections at the tournament (ever‑shared % → mean AW‑JOI90, ρ = +0.37, p = 0.039). And pairs that had ever shared a pitch averaged AW‑JOI90 = 2.93 vs 2.71 for pairs that hadn't (Mann‑Whitney p = 0.005). Real, if small at the pair scale.
  • What wins tournaments is network depth. Team chemistry density — many strong pairs distributed across the lineup — predicts WC22 finish at ρ = +0.76, sharply better than FIFA Overall (+0.55) and any shared‑time proxy. The four densest networks were the four semifinalists, in finish order: France, Croatia, Argentina, Morocco.
  • The story. Shared time helps build chemistry. Chemistry, when it's distributed densely across the squad, helps teams win. The transformer measures both halves.
Method & sources

Strong AW‑JOI pairs are same‑team WC22 pair‑seasons with attention‑weighted JOI per 90 ≥ 0.4 — the same metric and threshold used on the Team networks leaderboard. AW‑JOI itself is the per‑frame product of the two players' ball‑attention multiplied by max(ΔPscore, 0), accumulated across the tournament. See methodology §attention for the full pipeline (frame‑VAEP heads, score‑specialist transformer, ball‑distance baseline).

Prior shared minutes are computed pre‑WC22 from PFF rosters and the player_club_history_wc22 table: every minute any two WC22 squad‑mates had played together on the pitch at any club or national fixture before 2022‑11‑20 (Qatar kickoff). Estimated as overlap_seasons × 2,500 min. Biased by per‑league roster‑history coverage — see §3 for caveat.

Tournament result rank: winner=1, RU=2, 3rd=3, 4th=4, QF losers ranked 5–8 by group‑stage points, R16 losers 9–16, group‑stage exits 17–32 by points then goal diff. Standard FIFA tournament ranking.
