Chemistry, time, and the WC22 finish order

Football is different. There are no plays — chemistry is being predictable to a teammate and unpredictable to an opponent. We measure that off the ball with frame‑based AW‑JOI (the same metric ranking Messi and Argentina at the top of Team networks), then ask two questions: does chemistry predict tournament finish? and does pre‑tournament shared time predict chemistry?

Headline result. Across 31 WC22 sides, team chemistry density — the count of strong AW‑JOI pairs in each squad — predicts tournament finish at Spearman ρ = +0.76 (p < 0.001). The four densest chemistry networks in the tournament were the four semifinalists, in finish order: France, Croatia, Argentina, Morocco. Brazil (heavy bonds, fewer of them) shows the trade‑off — high per‑pair intensity, narrower distribution, out in the quarters.

What about time together? The most surprising result on this page: it doesn't help, and may even hurt. Summed national‑team caps per squad (Wikipedia, uniform coverage across all 32 nations) correlate negatively with chemistry density: ρ = −0.36 (p = 0.045). Tournament finish vs total caps: ρ = −0.31 (p = 0.09). The high‑caps teams (Qatar 1,472, Belgium 1,357, Mexico 1,317) are smaller talent pools that recycle the same XI every cycle; the strong‑chemistry semifinalists (Argentina 887, France 892, Croatia 979, Morocco 521) rotate larger pools and build chemistry through tactical fit, not raw match count.

3. Does prior time together predict chemistry?

The surprise: more time together correlates with less chemistry. We pulled summed national‑team caps for every WC22 squad from Wikipedia's WC22 squads page — coarser than per‑pair shared minutes but with uniform coverage across all 32 nations. The result is consistent and significant:

  • Total team caps → chemistry density: ρ = −0.36, p = 0.045
  • Total team caps → tournament finish: ρ = −0.31, p = 0.09

High‑caps squads are small talent pools that recycle the same XI: Qatar (1,472 total caps, because they play AFC qualifying and Gulf Cup constantly), Belgium (1,357 — Hazard/De Bruyne/Lukaku have 100+ each), Mexico (1,317). The deep‑run teams rotate larger pools: Argentina 887, France 892, Morocco 521. Chemistry density isn't built up by playing more matches together — it emerges from tactical fit and per‑pair compatibility, which the model picks up directly.

Earlier metric, for reference. The PFF club‑history estimate (overlap_seasons × 2,500 min) was biased by roster‑history coverage: PL/BL/LL/SA players have complete career trails, others don't, so Germany's 308k figure was partly artifact. Coverage‑normalized version (ever‑shared % of known pairs) is weakly positive (ρ = +0.29 to chemistry density, p = 0.11). The caps result is the cleaner answer.

Each dot = one squad. X = total national‑team caps in the WC22 squad. Notice the inverse pattern: the four semifinalists (gold ring) sit toward the middle‑to‑low caps range, while the highest‑caps squads (Qatar, Belgium, Mexico, Uruguay) cluster at the bottom of the chemistry‑density axis.

Pair scale: essentially null either way. At the individual pair level (n = 3,443), the PFF prior‑shared‑minutes estimate barely moves chemistry: event‑level JOI90 ρ = +0.02, frame‑level AW‑JOI90 ρ = +0.05. Pairs that had ever shared a pitch averaged AW‑JOI90 = 2.93 vs 2.71 for pairs that hadn't (Mann‑Whitney p = 0.005). A real but tiny bump. You can't point to any one pair and say "they'll click because they've played together."

The takeaway. Chemistry density predicts WC22 finish at ρ = +0.76. None of the time‑together proxies we built — club‑history shared minutes, ever‑shared %, national‑team caps — break ρ = ±0.4 in either direction. Chemistry as the transformer measures it is its own thing: a tactical/positional property of the squad, not a function of how many minutes the players have spent together.

Method & sources

Strong AW‑JOI pairs are same‑team WC22 pair‑seasons with attention‑weighted JOI per 90 ≥ 0.4 — the same metric and threshold used on the Team networks leaderboard. AW‑JOI itself is the per‑frame product of the two players' ball‑attention multiplied by max(ΔPscore, 0), accumulated across the tournament. See methodology §attention for the full pipeline (frame‑VAEP heads, score‑specialist transformer, ball‑distance baseline).

Prior shared minutes are computed pre‑WC22 from PFF rosters and the player_club_history_wc22 table: every minute any two WC22 squad‑mates had played together on the pitch at any club or national fixture before 2022‑11‑20 (Qatar kickoff). Estimated as overlap_seasons × 2,500 min. Biased by per‑league roster‑history coverage — see §3 for caveat.

National‑team caps are scraped from the Wikipedia 2022 FIFA World Cup squads page, which lists each player's senior cap count as of the start of the tournament. Summed per squad (831 players, 32 nations). Coarser than PFF shared minutes but uniform coverage across nations.

Tournament result rank: winner=1, RU=2, 3rd=3, 4th=4, QF losers ranked 5–8 by group‑stage points, R16 losers 9–16, group‑stage exits 17–32 by points then goal diff. Standard FIFA tournament ranking.
