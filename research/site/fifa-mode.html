Chemistry, time, and the WC22 finish order

Football is different. There are no plays — chemistry is being predictable to a teammate and unpredictable to an opponent. We measure that off the ball with frame‑based AW‑JOI (the same metric ranking Messi and Argentina at the top of Team networks), then ask two questions: does chemistry predict tournament finish? and does pre‑tournament shared time predict chemistry?

Headline result. Across 31 WC22 sides, team chemistry density — the count of strong AW‑JOI pairs in each squad — predicts tournament finish at Spearman ρ = +0.76 (p < 0.001). The four densest chemistry networks in the tournament were the four semifinalists, in finish order: France, Croatia, Argentina, Morocco. Brazil (heavy bonds, fewer of them) shows the trade‑off — high per‑pair intensity, narrower distribution, out in the quarters.

And the mechanism is partly time. Pre‑tournament shared minutes (club + national, any venue) predicts a team's chemistry density at ρ = +0.43 (p = 0.017) — real, but only partial. Shared rehearsal time is one input to chemistry; it isn't the whole story. Morocco's 2022 squad had almost none of it and still ran fourth.

3. Does prior time together predict chemistry?

For each WC22 pair we computed prior shared minutes — every minute the two players were on the pitch together in any club or national fixture before WC22. Then we asked: does that shared history predict the chemistry we measure at the tournament, both at the event level (JOI90, who passes to whom) and the frame level (AW‑JOI90, who the model co‑watches off the ball)?

The honest answer: yes at the team scale, almost no at the pair scale.

  • Pair level (n = 3,443 pairs). Prior shared minutes is essentially uncorrelated with event‑level JOI90 (ρ = +0.02, p = 0.38) and barely correlated with frame‑level AW‑JOI90 (ρ = +0.05, p = 0.003 — significant only because n is large; the effect is tiny). Knowing two players' shared‑minutes history tells you almost nothing about how dense their pair will be at the World Cup.
  • Binary "ever played together" is a hair stronger. Pairs that had ever shared a pitch before WC22 averaged AW‑JOI90 = 2.93 vs 2.71 for pairs that hadn't (Mann‑Whitney p = 0.005). A real but small bump.
  • Team level (n = 31 squads). Total prior shared minutes does predict team chemistry density (ρ = +0.43, p = 0.017) and mean AW‑JOI90 (ρ = +0.48, p = 0.006). The signal exists at the squad scale, not the pair scale — teams whose players collectively share more rehearsal hours build denser networks, but you can't point to any one pair and say "they'll click because they've played together."

The eight squads with the most pre‑WC22 shared minutes were Germany, England, Spain, Brazil, Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Netherlands, Portugal — and most exited before the semis. So shared rehearsal time correlates with chemistry, but it isn't the recipe. Morocco's 2022 squad had almost none of it and still ran fourth.

Why pair‑level history fails and team‑level history succeeds. Two players sharing a club for years guarantees nothing about WC chemistry — managers play different systems, partnerships rotate, and dense on‑ball connections need both players in compatible roles. But a squad with many partially‑shared histories has many chances to find one or two pairs that click, and that's what shows up in n_strong_pairs. Chemistry density is an emergent property of the roster, not the sum of pair‑level shared minutes.

What this means for WC26. Paper ratings overweight top‑loaded squads with marquee names. Chemistry density rewards squads with many dense pair‑connections distributed across the lineup. Different shapes work — but denser networks consistently advance further.

Method & sources

Strong AW‑JOI pairs are same‑team WC22 pair‑seasons with attention‑weighted JOI per 90 ≥ 0.4 — the same metric and threshold used on the Team networks leaderboard. AW‑JOI itself is the per‑frame product of the two players' ball‑attention multiplied by max(ΔPscore, 0), accumulated across the tournament. See methodology §attention for the full pipeline (frame‑VAEP heads, score‑specialist transformer, ball‑distance baseline).

Prior shared minutes are computed pre‑WC22 from PFF rosters and the player_club_history_wc22 table: every minute any two WC22 squad‑mates had played together on the pitch at any club or national fixture before 2022‑11‑20 (Qatar kickoff).

Tournament result rank: winner=1, RU=2, 3rd=3, 4th=4, QF losers ranked 5–8 by group‑stage points, R16 losers 9–16, group‑stage exits 17–32 by points then goal diff. Standard FIFA tournament ranking.
