Chemistry, time, and the WC22 finish order

Football is different. There are no plays — chemistry is being predictable to a teammate and unpredictable to an opponent. We measure it off the ball with frame‑based AW‑JOI (the metric that ranks Messi and Argentina at the top of Team networks) and then tell two halves of one story: shared time builds chemistry → dense chemistry wins tournaments.

Dense chemistry networks predict WC22 finish

n_strong_pairs counts pairs on the same squad with attention‑weighted JOI per 90 ≥ 0.4 — the number of dense, repeatable connections inside the team. The metric is built from the off‑ball attention of our tracking transformer (see methodology and the leaderboards). The four densest chemistry networks in WC22 were the four semifinalists, in finish order: France, Croatia, Argentina, Morocco.

Time together helps build chemistry

Shared playing history is one of the inputs to chemistry. We measure it two ways. Both give the same picture: teams that recruit from clusters of shared clubs build denser chemistry networks in Qatar.

History Index. The count of WC22 squad players who have at least one current or former club‑mate inside their own national squad. A coarser, more interpretable measure than estimated minutes. France: 20 of 24 squad players. Spain: 19 of 20. Argentina: 18 of 24. Portugal: 19 of 24. These are squads with overlapping club careers — the kind of group that arrives at the tournament already understanding each other.

Time → Chemistry → Winning. The arc this site argues for, in three steps:

  • Shared time builds chemistry. Squads with high History Index (France 20, Spain 19, Argentina 18, Portugal 19) build denser networks: ρ = +0.36 to chemistry density (p = 0.045), ρ = +0.38 to mean AW‑JOI90 (p = 0.034). And pairs that had ever shared a pitch averaged AW‑JOI90 = 2.93 vs 2.71 for pairs that hadn't (Mann‑Whitney p = 0.005).
  • Dense chemistry wins tournaments. Team chemistry density predicts WC22 finish at ρ = +0.76 — much better than FIFA Overall (+0.55) or any shared‑time proxy directly. The four densest networks were the four semifinalists, in finish order.
  • The transformer sees both halves. Shared‑club history is an input; chemistry density is the output; tournament finish is what it predicts. The model isn't measuring resumes — it's measuring how well a squad fits together on the pitch right now.
Method & sources

Strong AW‑JOI pairs are same‑team WC22 pair‑seasons with attention‑weighted JOI per 90 ≥ 0.4 — the same metric and threshold used on the Team networks leaderboard. AW‑JOI itself is the per‑frame product of the two players' ball‑attention multiplied by max(ΔPscore, 0), accumulated across the tournament. See methodology §attention for the full pipeline (frame‑VAEP heads, score‑specialist transformer, ball‑distance baseline).

Prior shared minutes are computed pre‑WC22 from PFF rosters and the player_club_history_wc22 table: every minute any two WC22 squad‑mates had played together on the pitch at any club or national fixture before 2022‑11‑20 (Qatar kickoff). Estimated as overlap_seasons × 2,500 min. Biased by per‑league roster‑history coverage — see §3 for caveat.

Tournament result rank: winner=1, RU=2, 3rd=3, 4th=4, QF losers ranked 5–8 by group‑stage points, R16 losers 9–16, group‑stage exits 17–32 by points then goal diff. Standard FIFA tournament ranking.
