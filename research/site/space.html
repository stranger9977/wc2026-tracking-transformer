What is space? What is control?

Almost every goal starts as space — a yard a defender didn't cover, a lane that opens for half a second. The sides that manufacture the most of it create the most chances and win the most games. Before we measure who's best at it, two words worth pinning down — in plain language, then in FIFA's own 2026 World Cup numbers so you can see them, not just hear them.

Space is the controllable pitch a team can actually use. Not the empty green between bodies — the grass a team can get to and do something with: the room they stretch open between an opponent's lines, or the gap they spring into behind the last defender. Good teams manufacture it; they pull markers apart and step into the seam.

Control is whose influence owns a patch of grass. Drop a ball onto any spot and ask: which team would get there first? That answer — repeated across every square metre — is pitch control. Space is the controllable pitch; control is the map of who'd win each piece of it.

The shape a team occupies — Brazil 1–1 Morocco, WC2026

Each block is the pitch a team's outfield players actually occupy in a phase — width across, length up the pitch, straight from FIFA's Post-Match Summary Report. Reaching into space — offers to receive in behind, per match (live WC2026)

FIFA now counts, every match, how often a teammate offers to receive in behind the back line — a runner sprinting into the space beyond the last defender, asking for the ball. It is space made countable: more offers in behind means a team is repeatedly attacking the most valuable grass on the pitch. Spain and Uruguay lead the early tournament.

FIFA's Enhanced Football Intelligence already counts space this way — line-breaks, offers to receive in behind, phases of play — back in 2022 and live again for 2026 (which adds a value-laden threat number on top). But what EFI hands you are match totals: how many times, not where or when. The acts below add the layer the totals can't — the per-frame surface reconstructed from raw tracking: who controls which patch of grass, and the danger pocket forming before the pass. Same idea of space, two granularities — and the study ends where the live 2026 feed begins.

0 Where the danger lives

Before space, the value of space.

Expected Threat (xT) — Karun Singh's canonical model — scores every spot on the pitch by one question: if your team has the ball here, how likely does it become a goal within the next few actions? It is learned from millions of recorded possessions. Move the ball into a higher-xT zone and you have added threat — no shot required yet.

A worked example: a player in a midfield pocket sits at roughly 0.02 threat — the ball there rarely becomes a goal soon. Slide it forward into the central pocket at the top of the box and that spot is worth about 0.26. The pass itself added +0.24 of threat, purely by moving the ball to more valuable grass.

What does an xT of 0.26 mean — and how is it different from xG?
  • It's a 0–1 value like a probability, but it is not xG. xG scores a single shot — "how likely is this shot to be a goal?" xT scores possession at a place — "if our team has the ball here, how likely is a goal within the next few actions?" xT exists for every spot on the pitch; xG only exists once someone shoots.
  • That's why 0.26 looks high. A shot from the top of the box is only a few percent to score (its xG). But possession there is worth ~0.26 xT — far more — because xT counts everything that can still happen: the cutback, the second ball, the better shot two passes later. xG values the shot; xT values the situation.
  • Which box: the peak is the central zone at the top of the 18-yard penalty box (the “D”) — not the six-yard box. Straight in front of goal actually scores lower here, because in open play the ball rarely arrives and settles there; threat lives where teams reliably build a shot.

1 Defensive Gravity

Who bends the back line.

Pure space, no goals: how many markers an attacker ties up (drawn), amplified by how hard those defenders sprint at him (chase-pull). Target men and focal attackers warp the most; roaming creators like Messi score lower because they slip between markers instead of pinning them. The surface is the defender pitch-control field — watch the block collapse toward the gravity attacker and a lane tear open.

So what? — the xG receipt

Team gravity (x) vs xG for, per match (y). A mild positive link (ρ=+0.38) — suggestive, not proof. The scatter is the evidence: teams that bend the block more tend to create a little more, but the spread is wide.

How this is measured
  • Space-native by construction: gravity is geometry (drawn markers × inward defender chase), never xT- or goal-weighted. The engine is used only for the hero defender-control surface.
  • The xG link is team-level over 12 teams — directional, correlation not causation.
  • 10 star-heavy / knockout matches at ~2 Hz; opponent and game-state shape every number.
  • Velocity (hence chase-pull) is finite-differenced at ~2 Hz; sub-second closing bursts are averaged out.
  • Tie-aware: when values overlap, players share a rank rather than forcing a single ordering.

2 Dangerous Space

Dangerous Space.

(pitch control × Expected Threat; academically, pressure-aware OBSO)

This is the strong one. It finds the off-ball player who controls the most valuable grass — space the ball could reach that's also close to a goal (high xT). Control over low-value grass is nothing; control over the danger pocket is everything. Scrub the auto-selected off-ball run from the Argentina–France final: a bright danger-pocket blooms ahead of the run, before the pass exists.

Dangerous-space surface — an off-ball run into the danger zone (ARG v FRA final)

controlled but low-valuecontrolled danger

The runner is highlighted and labelled on the clip by default — ring plus name tag — so the danger-maker is never lost in the dots. Toggle reveal danger to fade the ball-centric view and keep only cells that are both controlled and threatening (× xT) — you watch the scoring opportunity form ahead of the run, before the pass.

Can't a player just stand in valuable space and rack this up?
  • No — it measures pitch CONTROL, not presence. Think hoops: standing under the basket is worth nothing if a big body is between you and the ball — he gets there first. Same here. Every cell is weighted by who would actually reach the ball there first, given everyone's position and momentum (Fernández–Bornn pitch control). If a defender is closer or goal-side, he owns that grass and the attacker's value there is ~0. You only score the space you'd genuinely win.
  • Marked = not yours. A striker in the six-yard box with a centre-back goal-side of him controls almost none of it. Peel off into a lane nobody covers and the same spot lights up — that off-ball timing is exactly what the metric is built to catch.
  • It's also xT-weighted and pressure-aware: controlling low-value grass is worth nothing, and control under heavy pressure is discounted.
  • The honest edge: if a player really is unmarked in the danger zone, he is credited — and he should be. Being uncovered in dangerous space is the valuable thing; ghosting into it unmarked is the skill, not a loophole.

So what? — the xG receipt (the strong one)

Team danger-moments per minute (x) vs StatsBomb xG per match (y). The trend is real: ρ=+0.71, p=0.022, n=10. Owning dangerous space tracks chances.

How this is measured
  • Team xG uses regulation time (penalty-shootout goals excluded); the flow (danger-moments per minute) tracks xG, while the static stock does not.
  • Dangerous-space stock (mean controlled-danger area per frame) barely separates teams (63.7–66.4) — the team board ranks the flow (danger-moment rate) instead.
  • The player headline rests on present-frames-only averaging, which favours late-game attacking subs; that is why cameo subs are split into their own tier and the qualified-minutes group leads.
  • Possession and carrier identity are heuristic (nearest-player-to-ball), not event-joined. Velocity is finite-differenced at ~2 Hz. Extra time is excluded from both tracking and xG.

★ The full toolkit · 2022 → live 2026

Two lenses on the same space — then live in 2026.

Everything above is one half of a toolkit: our frame-by-frame reconstruction from PFF tracking. The other half is FIFA's own Enhanced Football Intelligence — the official space metrics in every match report. On the 2022 final, two systems that never saw each other's data tell the same story. And for 2026 the EFI half is already live — the tracking half we'll bring play by play.

Argentina 3–3 France — the 2022 final, measured two ways

Left, FIFA's official EFI, read straight off the match-report PDF; right, our pitch-control reconstruction from raw tracking. Two measurement systems, no shared data. Argentina broke more lines, received far more in the final third, moved into the space behind more often — and held more dangerous space per minute. France's danger ran through one man: Mbappé's hat-trick, and the danger-pocket you scrubbed in Act 2. Same final, same story.

The same space metrics, live — World Cup 2026 so far

Straight from FIFA's feed, no tracking required: completed line breaks and offers to receive in behind — the very metrics the 2022 final was measured on, above — now updating each matchday for the tournament in progress.

The tracking half, for 2026: we can't run pitch control across a whole tournament with no public 30 Hz feed — but computer vision can pull a few example plays from broadcasts, and we'll run these metrics — defensive gravity and dangerous space — on them, exactly as the acts above do for 2022. The acts you just scrubbed are the prototype; CV-tracked clips are the production line as the matches come.
