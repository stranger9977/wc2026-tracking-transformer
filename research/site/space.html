· Foundations

What is space? What is control?

Almost every goal starts as space: a yard a defender didn't cover, a lane that opens for half a second. The whole game is a fight over it. Teams work to control the space that matters, the grass near the goal they can actually reach, and the side that owns more of it creates more chances and scores more goals. Two words are worth pinning down first. Then we measure who is best at it.

Space is the controllable pitch a team can use. It is not the empty green between bodies. It is the grass a team can reach and do something with: the room they stretch open between an opponent's lines, or the gap behind the last defender they spring into. Good teams manufacture it. They pull markers apart and step into the seam.

Control is whose influence owns a patch of grass. Drop a ball onto any spot and ask which team gets there first. Repeat that across every square metre and you have pitch control. Space is the controllable pitch. Control is the map of who would win each piece of it.

Owning space only matters if it is space worth owning. Next we put a number on every patch of grass: how much each spot raises a team's chance of scoring.

1 Where the danger lives

How do we value space on the pitch?

Expected Threat (xT) is the answer. It scores every spot on the pitch by one question: if your team has the ball here, how likely is it to become a goal within the next few actions? The model learns that answer from millions of recorded possessions. Move the ball into a higher-xT zone and you have added threat, no shot required.

Method: Karun Singh, Introducing Expected Threat (xT).

The gradient is steep. A midfield pocket is worth about 0.02 xT, the edge of the box about 0.11, and a central spot right in front of goal about 0.26. A pass that carries the ball from midfield to the goal mouth adds roughly +0.24 of threat just by moving it to more valuable grass. Read 0.26 as a probability: about a 26% chance the possession ends in a goal within the next few actions, starting from that spot. That is what makes xT different from xG. xG scores a single shot once someone takes it. xT scores possession at a place, and it exists for every spot on the pitch, peaking right in front of goal.

Where that number comes from: Mbappé vs Messi

A player's total is a long tail of small progressions, not a handful of line-breakers. About 40% of passes go backward or sideways and add no threat, so they count as 0. Each completed pass or carry adds its xT gain (a box entry ≈ +0.10, a square ball ≈ +0.005), and the total is just those bumps summed. Two routes reach the same ≈ 2.8:

Both land at ≈ 0.40 xT/match (total ÷ games) with opposite fingerprints. Mbappé gets there through fewer, sharper vertical balls, with most of his value in the moderate and big buckets. Messi gets there through sheer involvement, a huge pile of small progressions. xT-created rewards both the rare line-breaker and the relentless progressor. Read it as who moved the ball into danger most, not a goal count.

Two ways to value space

The other lens: V — value is where the defence guards

xT answers "where could a goal come from", so it only lights up near the opponent's goal. The Fernández–Bornn paper takes the opposite view: space is valuable where the defence bothers to guard it. Their value model V is a neural net trained on exactly that — given where the ball is, how much defensive coverage piles onto each part of the pitch. It inverts xT: the crowded build-up grass in midfield becomes valuable, not just the box. We trained it on World Cup tracking data.

Same ball, two value models. xT (left) never moves — the danger always lives at the goal. V (right) follows the ball: as it climbs the pitch, value re-pools around where defenders typically sit given the ball there. xT cares where the ball could end up; V cares where the play is being contested now.

2 Who owns the grass

Method: Fernández & Bornn (2018), “Wide Open Spaces: a statistical technique for measuring space creation in professional soccer” (MIT Sloan). Every control number on this page comes from this model.

Pitch control: who gets there first.

xT tells you where the danger is. Pitch control tells you who owns it. Picture a loose ball dropped onto one spot. Which player reaches it first? Not by distance alone, because a sprinting defender beats a standing striker who is two metres closer. Account for everyone's position and momentum, run that race for every square metre, and you get a control surface: a live map of which side would win each piece of the grass. It is the engine under every player metric that follows.

Each player owns a patch of reach around them, the grass they could realistically get to. Fernández & Bornn model that patch as a soft blob, brightest at the player and fading with distance. Two things reshape it. Speed stretches it: a player sprinting forward can reach grass ahead of them, so the blob lengthens in the direction they are moving, like the cone of a headlight. Distance to the ball sets its size: far from the ball the blob spreads wide, because there is time to cover ground before the ball ever arrives, and close to the ball it tightens to where they stand right now.

A spot's control compares the two sides' reach there. Add up the attacking blobs, subtract the defending blobs, and pass the result through a logistic curve to land between 0 and 1. A value near 1 means the team in possession clearly owns that grass. Near 0.5 means it is genuinely contested. Drag the dots below to feel it: move a player, flick it to add speed, and watch the field tilt.

The jargon, decoded. You will see this engine named a “bivariate-normal influence” model. Three plain words hide under that label:

Influence
how much a player owns a spot — total at his feet, fading with distance. Every player casts one of these.
Bivariate normal
the shape of that ownership: a bell-shaped mound over the pitch (“bivariate” just means two dimensions, x and y), tallest at the player and sloping down all around — the “soft blob” above.
Geometry
the mound is not a round hill: speed stretches it into an ellipse along the run, direction rotates it, and distance to the ball scales it.

Then control is a contest: sum the attackers' mounds, subtract the defenders', squeeze through the logistic to land in 0–1. A lone player with no support reads 0.73, not 1 — control needs density, not just presence.

So how much of the pitch does a team control over a game?

A control surface is one instant. Average it over a whole match and you get the honest version of “France controlled 55% of the pitch.” Two reads, across all 64 games: territorial control — the share of the grass a team owns outright (its influence beats the opponent's) — and dangerous space, the ball-aware OBSO it generates going forward (reach × control × xT).

Territorial control is the “controlled X% of the pitch” number — possession-dominant sides top it, and the two teams in a game always sum to 100%. Dangerous space is the OBSO a team generates attacking (reach × control × xT, xT-weighted m²·min) — per match is the per-game rate, total rewards deep runs. Leaders.

Pitch control aggregated per team across all 64 WC2022 games. Territorial control is possession-independent (measured for both teams every frame); dangerous space is ball-aware OBSO. The player version — who owns it (OBSO), who gains it by moving (SOG), and walking or running — is in Act 3.

3 Four applications of controlling valuable space

Controlling valuable space.

Acts 1 and 2 gave us two numbers for any spot on the pitch: xT for how much it is worth, and pitch control for who owns it. Multiply them and you have a single read on controlling space that is also dangerous. Run it across all 64 World Cup 2022 games and it answers four questions about individual skill. First, who owns the most dangerous space off the ball for himself — the stock (OBSO) — and who keeps gaining it by moving into value (Space Occupation Gain, SOG). Second, who creates it for teammates by dragging markers away (SGG). Third, who finds the player in that space with a pass. Fourth, who wins duels in the spots that are worth winning.

1 Application one · the off-ball read

Dangerous space: control x xT.

We borrow William Spearman's idea of control times value (he calls it OBSO) and compute it on our own Fernández-Bornn control surface.

This is the strongest read on the page, because it is the other two combined. It finds the off-ball player who controls the most valuable grass: space the ball could reach that also sits close to a goal. Control over a midfield pocket is worth almost nothing. Control over the space in front of goal is worth everything.

It scores control, not presence. A striker standing in the six-yard box with a centre-back goal-side of him owns almost none of it, because the defender reaches the ball there first. Every cell is weighted by who would win it first, given where everyone is and how fast they are moving. You only score the space you would actually win, and only the space that is dangerous: each square metre is scaled by its xT, so owning grass far from goal counts for nothing. The honest edge follows from this. A player who drifts unmarked into the danger zone is credited, and he should be, because being uncovered in front of goal is the skill the metric is built to catch.

Reading the number: it is square metres of grass, discounted twice — once by how much the player owns it (0–1 pitch control) and once by how dangerous it is (xT, which peaks near 0.26 right in front of goal and is ~0 back in midfield). A cell only counts near its full area when the player fully owns it and it sits in the danger zone. So En-Nesyri's ≈14 means that, frozen at any instant, he sits on the equivalent of ~14 m² of fully-owned, in-front-of-goal shooting space; most players sit near 5–8.

2 Application two · The decoy who frees a teammate

Creating space for someone else.

Application one credited the space a player wins for himself. This one credits the space he frees for a teammate. The move every coach talks about and no box score records: drag your marker away, and the man you left behind is suddenly open. Fernández & Bornn formalised it — when a defender who was marking one attacker gets pulled onto another within three seconds, releasing the first, the space the freed teammate gains is credited to the player who dragged the marker. It is the assist before the assist.

We run their detection across all 64 games. A generation event needs a defender within 5 m of a teammate, then within 5 m of the generator three seconds later and no longer near the teammate — a real drag, not incidental drift. The teammate's gain in owned dangerous space (control × xT, the same quantity as the off-ball board) is the credit. It rewards the pivots and decoys who bend a defence without ever touching the ball: Busquets dropping to pull a striker's marker, a forward's run that opens the channel behind him.

3 Application three · The passer who hits that space

The pass that hits the dangerous space.

Application one found the off-ball runner who drifts into valuable grass. This one finds the player who puts the ball there. A great pass does two things at once: it adds threat (moves the ball to a more dangerous spot — the same xT-added the clip tags on each pass) and lands somewhere keepable (controlled by his team at the target). The board below scores this two ways — progression (the threat each pass adds) and occupation (how much dangerous, controlled space a passer lives in) — so both the line-breakers and the deep controllers get their due. Who hits the dangerous, controlled space — two ways

Two questions, one board. Progression (default) scores control × xT(target) × xT-added: who threads the ball forward into more danger. Own-half build-up adds ≈0 threat, so it surfaces line-breaking creators (de Paul, Modrić, Messi), not centre-backs racking up volume. Occupation drops the xT-added term — control × xT(target) over every pass — so it rewards who lives in dangerous, controlled space, holding and recycling it: the deep-lying controllers (Rodri, Pedri, Kovačić) the progression view demotes because their passes don't move the ball to more danger. 4 Application four · Wins duels where it counts

Winning balls the percentages say you shouldn't.

Pitch control gives each player a baseline chance of winning a loose ball, from where he is and how he is moving: a true 50-50 is 50%, a better-placed player more. Winning above that baseline, again and again, is anticipation, timing and strength his positioning alone does not explain. We call it Balls Won Above Expected, and we weight each duel by the xT of where it happens. Scrub an auto-picked example: a genuine 50-50 won.

Pitch control is locked to the duel winner's team; both contestants are ringed (winner gold, the other red). The two arrive together and the model rated it a near-50-50 by position and momentum. Watch the winner come away with it. Winning more of these than the percentage predicts, summed over a tournament, is the skill the board measures.

Where the number comes from: one duel, step by step

Follow a single loose ball from the contest to the leaderboard number. Each step is one idea.

  1. 1
    A 50-50 happens. Two players chase the same loose ball. Here it is near the edge of the box — a dangerous spot.
  2. 2
    Pitch control sets the expectation. From where both players are and how fast they are moving, the model says our player should win it about 50% of the time. That is his baseline. expected = 0.50
  3. 3
    Did he actually win it? Yes. A win is 1, a loss is 0. actual = 1
  4. 4
    Above expected = actual − expected. He won a ball he was only meant to win half the time. 1 − 0.50 = +0.50 (lose a 50-50 and it is −0.50).
  5. 5
    Weight it by where it mattered. Multiply by the xT of that spot, so winning near goal counts and a clearance in your own half barely does. Near the box, xT ≈ 0.04. +0.50 × 0.04 = +0.020
  6. 6
    Add up every duel, per match. Do this for all his loose-ball duels and sum. Beating your baseline again and again, in dangerous spots is the skill — that running total, per game, is the leaderboard below.

Why one duel is small (≈ +0.02) but a tournament leader reaches ≈ +0.20 per match: it is a sum of many duels, and most are tiny — the number rewards volume of winning the right ones, not one big moment.

Wins his duels where it counts

Each ground duel is scored xT(where it happens) × (won − expected) — pitch control sets the expectation from both players' position and momentum, and the xT weight means winning a 50-50 near goal counts while a clearance in your own half barely does. Per match, weighted by opponent strength; group stage is the level field, knockout is who did it against the best. Leaders: the leaders.
