· Foundations

What is space? What is control?

Almost every goal starts as space — a yard a defender didn't cover, a lane that opens for half a second. The sides that manufacture the most of it create the most chances and win the most games. Before we measure who's best at it, two words worth pinning down — in plain language, then in FIFA's own 2026 World Cup numbers so you can see them, not just hear them.

Space is the controllable pitch a team can actually use. Not the empty green between bodies — the grass a team can get to and do something with: the room they stretch open between an opponent's lines, or the gap they spring into behind the last defender. Good teams manufacture it; they pull markers apart and step into the seam.

Control is whose influence owns a patch of grass. Drop a ball onto any spot and ask: which team would get there first? That answer — repeated across every square metre — is pitch control. Space is the controllable pitch; control is the map of who'd win each piece of it.

Reaching into space — offers to receive in behind, per match (live WC2026)

FIFA now counts, every match, how often a teammate offers to receive in behind the back line — a runner sprinting into the space beyond the last defender, asking for the ball. It is space made countable: more offers in behind means a team is repeatedly attacking the most valuable grass on the pitch. Spain and Uruguay lead the early tournament.

FIFA's Enhanced Football Intelligence already counts space this way — line-breaks, offers to receive in behind, phases of play — back in 2022 and live again for 2026 (which adds a value-laden threat number on top). But what EFI hands you are match totals: how many times, not where or when. The acts below add the layer the totals can't — the per-frame surface reconstructed from raw tracking: who controls which patch of grass, and the danger pocket forming before the pass. Same idea of space, two granularities — and the study ends where the live 2026 feed begins.

1 Where the danger lives

Before space, the value of space.

Expected Threat (xT) — Karun Singh's canonical model — scores every spot on the pitch by one question: if your team has the ball here, how likely does it become a goal within the next few actions? It is learned from millions of recorded possessions. Move the ball into a higher-xT zone and you have added threat — no shot required yet.

A worked example of the gradient: a midfield pocket is worth roughly 0.02 xT; the edge of the box about 0.11; a central spot right in front of goal about 0.26 — the value climbs the closer the ball gets to the goal. A pass that carries it from midfield to the goal mouth adds about +0.24 of threat, purely by moving it to more valuable grass.

What does an xT of 0.26 mean — and how is it different from xG?
  • It's a 0–1 value like a probability, but it is not xG. xG scores a single shot — “how likely is this shot a goal?” xT scores possession at a place — “if our team has the ball here, how likely is a goal within the next few actions?” xT exists for every spot on the pitch; xG only exists once someone shoots.
  • So 0.26 ≈ a 26% chance the possession ends in a goal within the next few actions (Singh's model settles after ~5), starting from that spot. It values the whole short sequence, not one shot — which is why it reads higher than a single shot's xG.
  • Where it peaks: right in front of goal — the central six-yard box / goal mouth — climbing the closer the ball gets to goal (midfield ~0.02 → edge of the box ~0.11 → front of goal ~0.26). The danger really is at the goal, steepest through the central channel.
  • These are Karun Singh's published values, not ours. The surface is his canonical open 12×8 xT grid (peak 0.2574); we verified our copy matches his published file exactly — the scale isn't something we invented.

2 Who owns the grass

Pitch control: who'd get there first.

xT tells you where the danger is. Pitch control tells you who owns it. Drop a ball onto any spot and ask which team would reach it first, given everyone's position and momentum — repeat that for every square metre and you have a control surface: a live map of which side owns each piece of the pitch. It's the engine under every player metric that follows.

We compute pitch control with the Fernández & Bornn (2018) model — “Wide Open Spaces.” Each player casts a bivariate-normal influence zone centred slightly ahead of them (nudged by their velocity), wider when the ball is far, tighter when it's close. A spot's control is a logistic of the attacking influence there minus the defending influence: ≈1 means the in-possession team clearly owns it, ≈0.5 means it's contested.

Which pitch-control model — Fernández–Bornn, not Spearman

There are two canonical pitch-control models, and they're easy to mix up — so to be unambiguous:

The two models, and which one we run
  • Fernández & Bornn (2018), “Wide Open Spaces” — this is what we use, everywhere. Control comes from each player's bivariate-normal influence (position + velocity). Every control number on this page — the surface above, the three player metrics below, the team scatter — is computed this way, in our pitch_control.py.
  • Spearman (2018), “Beyond Expected Goals” — the other model; we don't use it to compute control. It models control as a race: the probability each player can physically intercept the ball first (time-to-intercept under a motion model). It's what the popular Friends-of-Tracking / LaurieOnTracking code implements. We link it as the alternative, for comparison.
  • The one Spearman idea we borrow is OBSO (Off-Ball Scoring Opportunity = control × xT). That's a concept, not his control model — and in Way 3 below we compute it on our Fernández–Bornn control surface. So: F&B for all control; Spearman only for the name and shape of the dangerous-space idea.

So what? — control is table stakes; conversion is the skill

Zoom out to teams. Across all 64 games the contenders cluster in a tight 30–35% final-third-control band — owning space barely separates anyone (which is why a raw control ranking is uninteresting). What separates teams is whether they convert it. Final-third control (x) vs StatsBomb xG per match (y): above the line = more xG than control predicts (clinical / counter) — Portugal and England turn middling control into ~2.3 xG; Spain (0.17) and Croatia (0.40) own space without product. Owning the grass is necessary; cashing it in is the skill the next act measures, player by player.

How this is measured
  • Pitch control is the Fernández–Bornn surface; "final-third control" is the in-possession team's mean share of control where x > 17.5 m, over the sampled frames.
  • All 64 WC2022 matches at ~2 Hz; "plays above / below" is the residual of xG on final-third control (n=32 teams). Suggestive, not causal.

3 Three ways it becomes a player skill

The same model, turned on individuals.

Pitch control is usually a team heat-map for one moment. Turn it on players, across all 64 World Cup 2022 games, and it answers concrete questions about individual skill. Three of them — each with a clip and a leaderboard, all built on the same Fernández–Bornn control surface: who finds dangerous space with his passing, who wins duels the percentages say he shouldn't, and — combining control with xT — who owns the most dangerous space off the ball.

Way one · Finds dangerous space with his passing

The pass that finds the right space.

A great pass isn't just completed — it puts the ball into grass that's both dangerous (high xT) and keepable (the receiver actually controls it). We score every final-third pass by control × xT at the target, and the players who do it most are the tournament's creators. Scrub the auto-picked example: a pass into the danger zone.

Dark = the opponent owns it, or it's controlled but low-value (midfield). Watch the pocket bloom at the receiver before the ball arrives.

How it's calculated
  • The question: when a player passes into the attacking third, does the ball go into space that is both dangerous and keepable — not a hospital ball into traffic?
  • Pitch control at the target (Fernández–Bornn model): every player projects a bivariate-normal "influence" zone — nudged ahead of them by their velocity, ~4–10m wide depending on distance to the ball. The receiving team's control of the target spot is a logistic of (sum of attackers' influence − sum of defenders' influence) there: ≈1 means the receiver is in clear space, ≈0.5 means tightly contested.
  • Expected Threat (xT) at the target (Karun Singh's grid): how dangerous that spot is.
  • Per pass: value = control × xT at the intended target, counted only for passes whose target sits in the attacking final third. Per player: the sum across all such passes — so it rewards doing it often and well. Goalkeepers excluded.
  • Reads as: who repeatedly threads the ball into dangerous, uncontested final-third space — creators, not recyclers.

All 64 WC2022 games. The final-third restriction is what surfaces the creators — the top names — rather than deep ball-playing defenders, who rack up "progression" simply by passing forward out of their own half.

Way two · Wins the ball against the odds

Winning balls the percentages say you shouldn't.

Pitch control also says who should reach a contested ball first. A player who keeps winning 50-50s above that probability is beating his own positioning — anticipation, timing, strength. We call it Balls Won Above Expected. Scrub the biggest auto-picked upset: a duel won against the odds.

Pitch control is locked to the duel winner's team and the winner is ringed and labelled. The model gave the other player the edge on this ball — watch the winner get there anyway. That residual, summed over a tournament, is the skill the board measures.

Final-third ground duels · win rate above expected
How it's calculated
  • The idea: pitch control says who should reach a contested ball first; a player who wins 50-50s above that probability is beating his positioning — anticipation, timing, technique.
  • Each ground duel (PFF challenge events; headers excluded by requiring the ball below 1m): we take the two contestants' Fernández–Bornn influence at the ball — their position and speed pointed at it — and form an expected win probability: P(A wins) = influenceA ÷ (influenceA + influenceB). That's literally "the percentage says he should win it."
  • Then: actual (1 if he won the duel, 0 if not) − expected, averaged over all his duels. Positive = wins more than predicted.
  • Final-third version restricts to duels in the attacking third (attacking direction inferred from each team's goalkeeper position). It pools winning the ball both as a carrier (shielding / dribbling out) and as a challenger (tackling).

Per-duel residual — actual minus expected win rate — across all 64 games.

Way three · Dangerous space — the two combined

Dangerous Space: control × xT.

This is Act 1 (xT) × Act 2 (pitch control) multiplied together — the synthesis. Academically it's Spearman's pressure-aware OBSO, computed on our Fernández–Bornn control surface.

The strongest read of the three. It finds the off-ball player who controls the most valuable grass — space the ball could reach that's also close to a goal (high xT). Control over low-value grass is nothing; control over the danger pocket is everything. Scrub the auto-selected run from the Argentina–France final: Di María ghosts into a danger-pocket that blooms ahead of his run, receives, and scores — the metric lighting up the exact space the goal came from, before the pass exists.

Dangerous-space surface — an off-ball run into the danger zone (ARG v FRA final)

dark = opponent's, or low-valuebright = controlled danger

Reading the surface: it's control × xT, so a cell only glows when it's both controlled by the attacking team and valuable (near goal). Dark means one of those is ~zero — either the opponent owns it, or it's controlled but far from goal (midfield is low-value, so it stays dark even when Argentina owns it). Greenish = controlled but only modest value; the bright bloom by the goal is controlled and dangerous. The runner is ringed and labelled so he's never lost in the dots. Toggle reveal danger to keep only the controlled-and-threatening cells — you watch the scoring chance form ahead of his run, before the pass.

Can't a player just stand in valuable space and rack this up?
  • No — it measures pitch CONTROL, not presence. Think hoops: standing under the basket is worth nothing if a big body is between you and the ball — he gets there first. Same here. Every cell is weighted by who would actually reach the ball there first, given everyone's position and momentum (Fernández–Bornn pitch control). If a defender is closer or goal-side, he owns that grass and the attacker's value there is ~0. You only score the space you'd genuinely win.
  • Marked = not yours. A striker in the six-yard box with a centre-back goal-side of him controls almost none of it. Peel off into a lane nobody covers and the same spot lights up — that off-ball timing is exactly what the metric is built to catch.
  • It's also xT-weighted and pressure-aware: controlling low-value grass is worth nothing, and control under heavy pressure is discounted.
  • The honest edge: if a player really is unmarked in the danger zone, he is credited — and he should be. Being uncovered in dangerous space is the valuable thing; ghosting into it unmarked is the skill, not a loophole.

Off-ball danger controllers

Substantial-minutes players lead; cameo subs (<15 min, one match) are shown separately so they don't headline. The substantial-minutes group — En-Nesyri, Mbappé, Petković, Messi — is the trustworthy read.

At team level this same idea — danger-moments per minute of possession — tracked xG beautifully among the ten knockout contenders (ρ=+0.71), then inverted across all 64 games (ρ=−0.31): counter-attacking sides rack up unconverted transition space and float to the top. We cut that board rather than ship a number that flips with the sample.

How this is measured
  • The player headline rests on present-frames-only averaging, which favours late-game attacking subs; that is why cameo subs are split into their own tier and the qualified-minutes group leads.
  • Possession and carrier identity are heuristic (nearest-player-to-ball), not event-joined. Velocity is finite-differenced at ~2 Hz. Extra time is excluded from both tracking and xG.

★ The full toolkit · 2022 → live 2026

Two lenses on the same space — then live in 2026.

Everything above is one half of a toolkit: our frame-by-frame reconstruction from PFF tracking. The other half is FIFA's own Enhanced Football Intelligence — the official space metrics in every match report. On the 2022 final, two systems that never saw each other's data tell the same story. And for 2026 the EFI half is already live — the tracking half we'll bring play by play.

Argentina 3–3 France — the 2022 final, measured two ways

Left, FIFA's official EFI, read straight off the match-report PDF; right, our pitch-control reconstruction from raw tracking. Two measurement systems, no shared data. Argentina broke more lines, received far more in the final third, moved into the space behind more often — and held more dangerous space per minute. The same space metrics, live — World Cup 2026 so far

Straight from FIFA's feed, no tracking required: completed line breaks and offers to receive in behind — the very metrics the 2022 final was measured on, above — now updating each matchday for the tournament in progress.

The tracking half, for 2026: we can't run pitch control across a whole tournament with no public 30 Hz feed — but computer vision can pull a few example plays from broadcasts, and we'll run these metrics — the passing, duel and dangerous-space reads above — on them, exactly as the acts above do for 2022. The clips you just scrubbed are the prototype; CV-tracked clips are the production line as the matches come. (FIFA's live threat board — the xT-cousin — is up in Act 1.)
