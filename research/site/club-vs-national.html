Club vs national: why does chemistry travel — or not?

Every four years, the World Cup creates the same puzzle. A striker who makes a Premier League defence look slow disappears for ninety minutes in a national-team shirt. A second-choice forward few outside his country had heard of becomes the player of the group stage. Some partnerships that look automatic at PSG or Bayern never quite click in blue, white, red. Others — assembled for three weeks every couple of years — outscore the sum of their parts.

The question this page asks is the central one Bransen & Van Haaren (MIT Sloan 2020) set up but never had the cross-context data to answer: how much of a national-team performance is the player, and how much is the partners around him? The leaderboards tell you which pairs clicked at WC22. This page asks the follow-up — did those players bring that chemistry from their clubs, or was the country a different story?

We do it in two steps. Step 1: find the players who out- and under-performed at WC22 itself, measured against a positional baseline of expected offensive impact (OI/90). That tells you who stood out at the tournament — but not why. A forward might out- perform because he is genuinely in form. He might out-perform because his national-team setup gives him better looks than his club does. Step 2: cross-reference every WC22 player against every other competition we have event data for — Euro 2024, Copa America 2024, partial Ligue 1 and Bundesliga seasons, and StatsBomb's parallel WC22 coverage. If a player out-performed at WC22 and out-performed at his club, it was form, not friction. If only at WC22, the national-team partnerships did something his club doesn't.

Caveats up front: club coverage is best for PSG, Bayern, and any player who also appeared at Euro/Copa 2024. Premier League, La Liga, and Serie A are largely missing for 2022/23. Treat absolute deltas as directional rather than precise; we say more about that in the

Step 1 · Form at the tournament

Δ OI/90 = a player's actual offensive VAEP per 90 at WC22 minus the OI/90 we'd expect from a same-position, same-minutes baseline built from every other WC22 player at that position. Positive Δ = the player out-played the median peer; negative Δ = below par. Filtered to players with ≥ 60 minutes total.

How we control for the competition gap

What's a z-score, in plain English? Take every forward playing in a competition. Compute their average OI/90 and how spread out the group is (the standard deviation σ). A z-score answers "how many σ above (or below) the average is this player?" A z of 0 means league-average for his role. +1 means "one σ above the typical forward in this competition." −1 means one σ below. The reason we need this: Bundesliga forwards average a lot more OI/90 than tournament forwards, so a +0.5 raw delta might really just be "your league has more OI on the table." Z-scores ask the harder question: relative to the players around you in the same competition, did you stand out more here than there?

Math / Python (technical audience) 
# research/scripts/compute_cross_context.py
def zscore(group_df, col):
    if len(group_df) < 5:           # too few peers to compare against
        return float("nan")
    mu = group_df[col].mean()
    sd = group_df[col].std(ddof=0) or 1.0
    return (group_df[col] - mu) / sd

# We group by (competition, role) and z-score within each cohort.
all_rows["z_oi_per90"] = (
    all_rows.groupby(["competition", "role"], group_keys=False)
    .apply(lambda g: zscore(g, "oi_per90"), include_groups=False)
)
# Δ z = z_other − z_wc22. Strips out league-wide OI inflation.
  • Raw Δ OI/90 — pure difference. Best for storytelling, worst for fairness: a Bundesliga forward averages more OI/90 than a tournament forward, so the raw Δ flatters anyone playing in a high-volume league.
  • Δ z-score (recommended) — within each (competition, role) we compute the OI/90 z-score (player minus the mean of same-role peers, divided by σ). Comparing z-scores across competitions strips out the league-wide scoring environment. A z of +1.5 in Bundesliga vs +0.5 at WC22 means "this player was much further ahead of his peer cohort at the club than at the tournament."
  • Δ OI / touch — switch denominator from minutes to ball touches. Players in possession-heavy teams (PSG, Bayern) inflate per-90 just by having the ball more; per-touch isolates the productivity of each on-ball action.
  • Still uncontrolled. Opponent strength (a Ligue 1 mid-table match is not WC22 quarter-finals) and tactical role (a player can be a 9 at his club but a 10 at his country). Those need team-Elo or per-match opponent ratings — flagged as next step.

Open questions for the video

Three testable hypotheses for the chemistry-friction narrative. Where we have the data, the chart is here; where we don't, the answer is which data we'd need.

1. Does offence improve faster than defence as the tournament progresses?

Intuition: attackers find each other once they've played together for a few matches. Defensive pairings — communication, line-holding, who steps up to press — should take longer because they require coverage of more pitch and tighter coordination.

We test it by computing the league-wide mean of P(score) (the VAEP value at each action of scoring within ten actions) and P(concede) per stage of the tournament. If offence grows and defence holds, you'd see P(score) rise from Group → Final while P(concede) stays flat or drops.

2. Do players take time to gel after a transfer?

We don't ship transfer-history data with this site, but the qualitative analogue is visible in the cross-context table: players whose first Premier League / Bundesliga / Ligue 1 minutes came shortly before WC22 should show a club-vs-WC22 lift that disappears in their next appearance at Euro/Copa 2024 once they've settled.

Data needed to test this cleanly: TransferMarkt / Wyscout transfer dates joined onto the cross-context table, and a per-club rolling window of OI/90 — first 10 league matches vs all subsequent. A player whose "new-club" window is well below his "settled" window is what we'd expect, and we'd want to see WC22 fall on the "new-club" side of the curve more often than chance.

What we can say from this dataset: Jude Bellingham (then 19, in his second Bundesliga season at Dortmund) shows a negative WC22-vs-club delta in z-space, while Bukayo Saka — a year deeper into his Arsenal regular minutes — shows a clear lift at WC22. Anecdotal, but consistent with the "new player still learning the team" story we'd want to test at scale.

3. How much of WC22 over/under-performance is just regression to the mean?

If a player's WC22 OI/90 is almost uncorrelated with his club OI/90, most of the WC22 result is noise plus national-team-specific factors (opponents, role, chemistry). If it's strongly correlated, the player is the player wherever he plays, and the "chemistry friction" story is mostly an artifact of small samples regressing toward each player's true mean. We compute the Pearson r over every player with ≥ 120 club minutes and ≥ 90 WC22 minutes.

This is the same null result you see on FIFA Mode — there, the OI/90-vs-FIFA-Overall fit has slope ≈ 0; here, the OI/90-vs-OI/90 fit has r ≈ 0. Paper talent doesn't predict tournament output. Club output doesn't predict it either. Something else is doing the work — the candidate is what teammates do for each other when the ball isn't there.

Does club rehearsal carry over?

The lore is suspicious. Spain 2010 (6 Barça starters) won. Germany 2014 (7 Bayern) won. Surely the rehearsed automatisms travel. We tested it on WC22 with two metrics — event-based JOI and the transformer's off-ball attention — and the two metrics literally disagree on the same teams.

Act 1 · The bold claim fails on JOI

For every WC22 same-team pair we know both players' club histories for, regress pair JOI90 against prior_shared_minutes:

  • Pooled (n = 3,774): Pearson r = −0.006 (p = 0.72). Flat.
  • Germany Bayern cluster (7 starters): Δ JOI90 = −0.101 — the worst.
  • Ajax Netherlands: −0.050 · Man City England: −0.048 · PSG/Bayern France: −0.035.

Thomas Müller had 90,000 prior minutes on the pitch with his WC22 teammates — the most in the dataset. His mean JOI90 at WC22: −0.015. Cluster size correlates inversely with progression among the elite: Qatar (4.1 shared teammates/player) and Saudi Arabia (4.0) both crashed out in the group stage; the least-clustered elite nation — Argentina, 1.8 — won.

Act 2 · One rich case survives

Argentina is the interesting case because by November 2022 their PSG cluster had already dissolved: Di María and Paredes had left for Juventus, leaving Messi as the only current PSG player. Cluster size at WC22 = 1.

But carve the squad by historical PSG ties (the 2018–22 triangle: Messi + Di María + Paredes):

Pairs with ≥1 ex-PSG playerJOI90 = 0.120 (n=40)
Neither ex-PSGJOI90 = 0.019 (n=89)

A 6× chemistry differential. Messi's top JOI partner at WC22 is Di María (J90 = 0.33, by far his highest). The on-ball rehearsal did travel — for those three players.

Act 3 · The tracking model disagrees

We trained a score-specialist transformer (single-head, dedicated to learning offensive geometry) and asked: among Argentina's same-team pairs, what's the score-specialist attention ratio for the PSG triangle vs the rest?

The off-ball model doesn't see the PSG triangle as special. Messi+Di María's score-specialist attention (2,862) is modest against a team mean of 2,530 — despite their JOI90 = 0.33 being the highest pair on the team. The PSG rehearsal is event-bound. It's "we pass to each other a lot," not "we're geometrically attuned off the ball."

The reversal: Germany Bayern

Germany was the worst JOI cluster — −0.101 below baseline. But on the score-specialist's tracking attention, Germany is the only nation with a clean significant positive shared-history signal:

Ever-shared club (Bayern core)attn 1,366 (n=29)
Never-sharedattn 894 (n=76)
Δ+471, Mann-Whitney p < 0.001

The two metrics disagree on the same team. The model's offensive geometry attends to Germany's Bayern cluster as if they should produce more — their shape looks like a scoring team. But the on-ball outcomes (JOI) say they didn't execute it. France PSG, Spain Barça, Argentina, Netherlands, England — all flip negative or null under the same test. Only Germany Bayern reverses.

The story isn't "rehearsal doesn't matter." It's "the model thinks rehearsal matters, the outcomes don't bear it out." Or put more sharply: Bayern Munich's 2022 Germany looked the part and didn't deliver.

Implications for 2026

The chemistry-friction signal is strongest for attackers and creative midfielders — players whose value depends on partnerships. Compare a striker's club OI/90 in his usual league with his national OI/90 at WC22, and you get a directional read on whether his country has assembled the pieces to play him. For 2026 the same player tracked across WC26 qualifying and his 2025/26 club season should give a 6-month look-ahead on who is "carrying chemistry" into the tournament.
